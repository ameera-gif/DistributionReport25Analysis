Key Terms Before You Start Reading

This report uses a few terms specific to MyFundAction's work. Here's what each one means, in plain language, so nothing below assumes prior knowledge.

BranchWhat MyFundAction calls each country office, Malaysia, Indonesia, Palestine, and so on. Each branch runs its own programs and reports its own spending.
QC4U (Qurban Care for Ummah)MyFundAction's Qurban program. Qurban is an Islamic practice, done mainly in one season each year, where an animal (cow, goat, or sheep) is sacrificed and the meat is given to people in need. QC4U is how MyFundAction organizes and delivers that.
RTE (Ready-to-Eat)Pre-made food packs handed directly to people, no cooking needed. Used when aid needs to reach a lot of people quickly, like in Palestine.
WASHShort for Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene. Covers programs like building water wells or clean-water systems.
Service touchOne instance of one person receiving one form of help, one meal handed out, one water delivery, one share of Qurban meat. If the same person receives aid three times, that's three "touches," not three people. This is why the ~236,000 figure is not the same as "236,000 different people."
DeduplicationThe process of checking whether the same person was counted more than once across different aid deliveries, so the final number reflects real, distinct people instead of total instances of help given. MyFundAction does not currently have a stated method for doing this.
$/touch (cost per touch)Total money spent divided by the number of service touches. A rough cost measure, only fair to compare between programs that work the same way, not across very different kinds of aid.
Restricted vs. unrestricted fundsRestricted money is donated for one specific purpose only, e.g. "only for Gaza", and legally cannot be spent elsewhere. Unrestricted money can be used anywhere the organization needs it. This report currently has no data on how much of MyFundAction's funding is one versus the other.
Overhead / admin cost ratioThe share of total spending that goes to running the organization itself, salaries, offices, admin, rather than directly to aid programs. Most charities report this figure; typical ranges are 10 to 20%. MyFundAction has not yet reported one.
Flat feeA cost that stays exactly the same no matter how much work was actually done. It's a red flag when it shows up unexpectedly, e.g. if a program costs "$12,000" every month regardless of whether it helped 500 people or 5,000, that's worth asking about.
Cash reserve / runwayHow many months an organization could keep operating using only its savings, if no new donations came in at all. A basic measure of financial safety that this report does not yet have data for.
IftarThe meal that breaks the daily fast during Ramadan. MyFundAction runs Iftar programs providing that meal to people who can't afford it.
FidyahA charitable payment in Islam, given (often as food) to compensate for a missed religious obligation. MyFundAction distributes Fidyah-funded aid on behalf of donors who owe it.
01

Where We Started

The context and constraints the organization entered 2025 with

MyFundAction entered 2025 running programs across seven places, Malaysia, Indonesia, Australia, USA, Middle East Africa, Palestine, and Turkiye. The work covers food security, WASH, health, education, disaster relief, and Qurban-based giving. This is a genuinely global charity, built up over several years.

Two things shaped where we started in 2025:

  • A donor base historically anchored in Malaysia, with Qurban-season giving (Apr to Jun) representing a recurring, large, and highly seasonal source of funds.
  • An active humanitarian emergency in Gaza, which by 2025 had already established Palestine as a major, ongoing programmatic commitment rather than a one-off crisis response.

This report has no 2024 numbers to compare against, no prior-year financials, no revenue history. That means the board is looking at 2025 as a first data point, not a trend. We know where the money went this year. We don't yet know if that's new, or just how things have always been.

🟡 Starting-point risk: Without a 2024 baseline or revenue history, the board cannot yet tell whether 2025's branch concentration (see Act 3) is a new development or a long-standing structural pattern.
› Given where we started, a global mission, a Malaysia-anchored donor base, and an active Gaza emergency, the next question is simple: what did we actually set out to do in 2025?

One Program Carried the Year

Inside Malaysia, one seasonal program, Qurban Care for Ummah (QC4U) (see Key Terms: this is MyFundAction's animal-sacrifice-and-meat-distribution program, run mainly in one season each year), spent $770,295. That's 41% of the whole organization's 2025 budget, spent in just three months (April to June).

Palestine Delivered at Scale

Palestine reached more people than any other branch, about 138,000 service touches. A 72,000-person Ready-to-Eat distribution in April drove most of that, followed by monthly hot-meal and clean-water deliveries the rest of the year.

Smaller Branches Stayed Small

USA, Australia, and Turkiye each spent well under 1% of the total budget, running small community activities (youth workshops, picnics, small iftar events) rather than large-scale relief. Was that the plan, or did these branches just not get funded? The data alone can't tell us.

Branch-by-Branch: What Each Number Actually Means

The table above shows what each branch spent and reached. It doesn't say what that number means for that branch specifically. Here's each one read on its own terms, not stacked against the others:

Malaysia — Our Anchor, Riding on One Program $1,348,839 · ~58,700 touches · 72% of FY spend

One program (QC4U) makes up 41% of the whole organization's budget, not just Malaysia's. Malaysia's health as a branch and the organization's seasonal risk are now the same question. A weak Qurban season wouldn't just hurt Malaysia, it would hurt our whole year.
Palestine — Reaches the Most People, Has the Thinnest Paperwork $398,400 · ~138,000 touches · 21% of FY spend

Reaches more people per dollar than any other branch. But several monthly costs come in at the same $12,000, regardless of how many people were reached, and the paperwork is thinner than Malaysia's. This is our highest-value branch to donors, and our highest-risk branch with auditors.
Middle East Africa — Small, and Genuinely Efficient $75,071 · ~17,200 touches · $4.37/touch

Second-best cost per person reached in the whole organization. But its water wells are priced the same flat fee no matter the size of the job, the same question we need to ask Palestine, just smaller here.
Indonesia — Our Best Number, Barely Mentioned $52,973 · ~19,200 touches · $2.76/touch

The best cost-per-person figure anywhere in the organization, yet it gets the least attention in this report. Worth finding out why this branch works so well before assuming we can copy it elsewhere.
USA, Australia, Turkiye — Small on Purpose, or Just Underfunded? Combined: $4,792, under 0.3% of the year's spend, small community activity only. Act 2 called this a deliberate "donor-engagement" role. Nothing in the actual 2025 data proves that, versus the simpler explanation that these branches just didn't get funded. Until we set a real success measure for what these branches are supposed to do, they'll keep getting judged against relief branches they were never meant to match.

Documentation Was Uneven

Photo and media evidence varied a lot by program, strong for Palestine's RTE program and Malaysia's QC4U, thin or missing for several Quran Distribution entries and the Nigeria/Uganda water wells.

🟡 The gap between plan and reality: Act 2 aimed for spread-out, steady impact. Act 3 shows heavy concentration driven by one season. That gap, not the numbers by themselves, is the real story of 2025, and it's what the board needs to dig into next.
› The gap between the plan and the results raises a harder question: what does this data actually prove, and what does it leave unanswered? That's where we need to look closer next.
04

What We Learned

What the results actually prove, and the limits of what they prove

Lesson 1: The Concentration Might Be About Funding, Not Choice

The 92.9% concentration in Malaysia and Palestine looks like a strategic imbalance. But without revenue data, we can't tell if we chose this, or if the money simply came in earmarked this way (restricted Qurban funds, Gaza-specific donations). This is the single biggest open question from the year.

Lesson 2: Our Impact Number Is Probably Inflated

The ~236,000 figure just adds up every program's count. In Palestine especially, the same households likely received RTE packs, hot meals, clean water, and Qurban meat, sometimes all in the same month. Without a method for removing duplicates, treat this number as total service touches, not verified unique people reached.

Lesson 3: You Can't Compare "Efficiency" Across Different Kinds of Programs

Malaysia's $23 per person looks worse than Palestine's $2.89. But a Qurban cow feeding dozens of people once isn't the same kind of thing as one RTE pack feeding one person once. The original "8 times less efficient" claim was a guess, not a proven fact, and should be labeled that way until someone actually checks it.

Here's why the comparison breaks: in Malaysia, one animal is bought once and its meat is shared across many households, so the "cost per person" divides a big one-time purchase across a lot of people, but it's still one big single expense. In Palestine, RTE packs are bought and handed out one at a time, so the cost naturally spreads out more evenly per person. Comparing the two "cost per person" numbers side by side makes it look like Malaysia is being wasteful, when really the two programs are built completely differently and were never meant to be measured the same way.

Lesson 4: We're Missing Half the Financial Picture

This whole analysis only looks at spending. We can't see revenue, overhead costs, cash reserves, or which funds are restricted. Any claim about "sustainability" right now is a guess, not a finding.

Lesson 5: One Year Isn't a Trend

What looked like a "front-loaded spending trend" is really just this year's seasonal pattern. Calling it a trend claims more than one year of data can actually support.

What Must Happen Next

Decisions, actions, and the questions that unlock them

Action Timeline

Immediate, Next 30 Days

  1. Request a full revenue statement by branch and restriction status, this unlocks nearly every other open question.
  2. Request the organization-wide admin/overhead cost ratio.
  3. Request management's beneficiary deduplication methodology, or confirmation that none currently exists.
  4. Correct the reporting-date anomalies (2026-dated entries inside the "2025" report).

Medium-Term, 3 to 6 Months

  1. Commission independent field verification for a sample of Palestine and MEA distributions, prioritizing the ~$128,000 in flagged, thinly-itemized spend.
  2. Begin building a rolling multi-year data set so 2026 can be measured against a genuine baseline.
  3. Diversify the fundraising calendar beyond Qurban season, but only once actual revenue concentration is confirmed, not assumed.

Long-Term

  1. Make a deliberate strategic choice: global brand with symbolic small-branch presence, or focused Malaysia + Palestine/MENA relief specialist, decide this only after revenue data clarifies whether today's concentration is a choice or a constraint.
  2. Build a contingency reserve fund once real reserve/runway figures are available.
  3. Introduce distinct KPI sets for relief-delivery branches versus donor-engagement branches (USA, Australia, Turkiye), so both are measured fairly against what they're actually meant to do.

What This Means, By Department

The same findings, translated into what each function should do differently

A report that only shows beneficiaries reached tells Operations something useful, and tells Marketing and Finance almost nothing. Below, the same 2025 findings are read again, this time through each department's own decisions.

MARKETING  Giving Calendar & Channel Effectiveness

Finding (from Acts 3–4)So What for Marketing
41% of FY spend, funded by giving, sits inside one 3-month Qurban windowThe acquisition calendar is almost certainly just as concentrated as the spend calendar. Map donor acquisition by month; if giving is this seasonal too, off-season campaigns aren't a nice-to-have, they're the only lever against cash-flow volatility.
0 revenue-by-source data points existWithout this, Marketing cannot know which channel, campaign, or appeal actually produced this year's donations. Every channel-effectiveness claim currently made is an assumption, not a measured result.
USA, Australia, Türkiye operate at donor-engagement scale, not relief scaleIf these branches are meant to build donor relationships rather than deliver aid, Marketing, not Operations, should own their success metric (reach, engagement, conversion to Malaysia/Palestine giving), not a beneficiary count that will always look small.

FINANCE  Cost Basis & Solvency

Finding (from Acts 3–4)So What for Finance
Multiple Palestine tranches show identical $12,000 costs regardless of beneficiary countThis is the single most auditable line in the whole report. Finance should confirm whether this is a genuine flat operating cost or a placeholder figure, before an external auditor asks the same question less kindly.
Flat-fee water wells carry ~$14,000 of similarly unexplained cost consistencySame issue, smaller scale. Worth resolving both at once, likely the same root cause (a standard quote used as an actual, or a cost genuinely fixed regardless of scope, either answer is fine, but it needs to be a stated answer).
Admin/overhead ratio and cash reserves: currently unreportedThese are two of the most basic solvency figures a board can ask for, and neither exists yet. This is Finance's most time-sensitive open item, everything else in this report is easier to act on once this exists.

OPERATIONS  Measurement Method, Not More Data Collection

Finding (from Acts 3–4)So What for Operations
~236,000 is a raw additive sum with no stated deduplication methodThis is an Operations-owned gap, not a Finance or Marketing one. A standard deduplication rule (even a conservative estimate, clearly labeled) is more defensible than an unlabeled inflated number.
Malaysia $23/touch vs. Palestine $2.89/touch was framed as an efficiency gapThis comparison should never have been made across program types. The fix is procedural: every project is measured against its own stated objective, not against another branch's unrelated program.
Documentation strength varies sharply by branch and programA monthly validation checkpoint, not a year-end scramble, is what would have caught Palestine's and MEA's documentation thinness while it was still fixable, not after the board already saw it.
✅ The bottom line: None of these three teams need a new report from Operations. They need this same data, read once through their own decisions. That change alone, not collecting more data, is the fastest improvement we can make before the next board cycle.

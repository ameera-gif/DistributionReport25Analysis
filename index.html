01

Where We Started

The context and constraints the organization entered 2025 with

MyFundAction entered 2025 as a multi-branch humanitarian organization operating across seven geographies, Malaysia, Indonesia, Australia, USA, Middle East Africa, Palestine, and Turkiye, with a mission spanning food security, WASH, health, education, disaster relief, and Qurban-based giving. This footprint reflects a genuinely global charitable identity built over prior years.

Two structural realities shaped the starting position for 2025:

  • A donor base historically anchored in Malaysia, with Qurban-season giving (Apr to Jun) representing a recurring, large, and highly seasonal source of funds.
  • An active humanitarian emergency in Gaza, which by 2025 had already established Palestine as a major, ongoing programmatic commitment rather than a one-off crisis response.

No 2024 baseline, prior-year financials, or revenue statements accompany this report, meaning the board is assessing 2025 as effectively a first data point, not a year-over-year comparison. This is an important starting constraint: we know where the money went in 2025, but not yet whether that represents continuity, growth, or a shift from prior years.

🟡 Starting-point risk: Without a 2024 baseline or revenue history, the board cannot yet tell whether 2025's branch concentration (see Act 3) is a new development or a long-standing structural pattern.
› Given this starting position, a global mission, a Malaysia-anchored donor base, and an active Gaza emergency, the natural next question is what the organization actually set out to accomplish in 2025.

Taken together, the intended strategy for 2025 was to convert a seasonal, Malaysia-heavy donor base into year-round, geographically diverse humanitarian delivery, using Qurban giving to fund livestock-based aid, while smaller branches (USA, Australia, Turkiye) served a complementary role in community engagement and fundraising visibility rather than large-scale relief delivery.

03

What Happened

The 2025 results, branch by branch, program by program

Spend Concentrated Sharply in Two Branches

Against the goal of diversified impact, actual 2025 spend concentrated heavily: Malaysia and Palestine together absorbed 92.9% of the $1,880,075 total budget. The remaining five branches shared roughly 7%.

One Program Carried the Year

Within Malaysia, a single seasonal program, Qurban Care for Ummah (QC4U), accounted for $770,295, or 41% of the organization's entire 2025 budget, concentrated in a three-month window (April to June).

2025 Spend Timeline, Seasonal Concentration

Palestine Delivered at Scale

The Palestine program reached the largest reported population, roughly 138,000 service touches, anchored by a 72,000-person Ready-to-Eat distribution in April, followed by recurring monthly hot-meal and clean-water tranches through the rest of the year.

Smaller Branches Stayed Small

USA, Australia, and Turkiye each operated at well under 1% of total budget, delivering community-scale activities (youth workshops, picnics, small iftar events) rather than large-scale relief. Whether this was by design (Act 2's "complementary role") or by funding shortfall is not distinguishable from the data alone.

Documentation Was Uneven

Verification quality (photo/media evidence) varied significantly, strong for Palestine's RTE program and Malaysia's QC4U, thin or absent for several Quran Distribution entries and Nigeria/Uganda water wells.

🟡 Gap between ambition and outcome: Act 2 aimed for diversified, sustained impact. Act 3 shows a heavily concentrated, seasonally-driven reality. That gap, not the numbers in isolation, is the central story of 2025, and it's what the board needs to interrogate next.
› The gap between the stated ambition and the actual results raises a harder question: what does this data really prove, and what does it leave unanswered? That's where scrutiny must now focus.
04

What We Learned

What the results actually prove, and the limits of what they prove

Lesson 1: Concentration May Reflect Funding Reality, Not Choice

The 92.9% concentration in Malaysia and Palestine looks like a strategic imbalance, but without revenue data, we cannot tell whether this reflects discretionary allocation or simply where the money came in tied to where it had to go (e.g., restricted Qurban funds, earmarked Gaza donations). This is the single most important open question from the year.

Lesson 2: Our Impact Number Is Likely Inflated

The ~236,000 beneficiary figure is an additive sum of program-level counts. In Palestine especially, the same households plausibly received RTE packs, hot meals, clean water, and Qurban meat within overlapping months. Absent a stated deduplication method, this number should be understood as total service touches, not verified unique people reached.

Lesson 3: "Efficiency" Comparisons Don't Hold Up Across Program Types

Malaysia's $23/touch looks inefficient next to Palestine's $2.89, but a Qurban cow feeding dozens of people once is not comparable to a single RTE pack feeding one person once. The original draft's "8x less efficient" framing was a hypothesis, not a proven finding, and should be labeled as such until validated.

Lesson 4: We Are Missing Half the Financial Picture

This entire analysis is built on expenditure data alone. We have no visibility into revenue, overhead/admin cost ratio, cash reserves, or restricted-vs-unrestricted funds. Any conclusion about "sustainability" or "dependency" is necessarily provisional.

Lesson 5: Trends Require More Than One Year

What looked like a "front-loaded, event-driven spending trend" is, on closer scrutiny, simply this year's seasonal pattern. Calling it a trend overstates what a single year of data can support.

05

What Must Happen Next

Decisions, actions, and the questions that unlock them

Action Timeline

Immediate, Next 30 Days

  1. Request a full revenue statement by branch and restriction status, this unlocks nearly every other open question.
  2. Request the organization-wide admin/overhead cost ratio.
  3. Request management's beneficiary deduplication methodology, or confirmation that none currently exists.
  4. Correct the reporting-date anomalies (2026-dated entries inside the "2025" report).

Medium-Term, 3 to 6 Months

  1. Commission independent field verification for a sample of Palestine and MEA distributions, prioritizing the ~$128,000 in flagged, thinly-itemized spend.
  2. Begin building a rolling multi-year data set so 2026 can be measured against a genuine baseline.
  3. Diversify the fundraising calendar beyond Qurban season, but only once actual revenue concentration is confirmed, not assumed.

Long-Term

  1. Make a deliberate strategic choice: global brand with symbolic small-branch presence, or focused Malaysia + Palestine/MENA relief specialist, decide this only after revenue data clarifies whether today's concentration is a choice or a constraint.
  2. Build a contingency reserve fund once real reserve/runway figures are available.
  3. Introduce distinct KPI sets for relief-delivery branches versus donor-engagement branches (USA, Australia, Turkiye), so both are measured fairly against what they're actually meant to do.

Questions to Ask Management, In Priority Order

  1. What was total 2025 revenue, by source and restriction status?
  2. Is ~236,000 unique individuals or total service touches, what's the deduplication method?
  3. What is our admin/overhead cost ratio versus sector benchmarks (typically 10 to 20%)?
  4. What are our cash reserves, in months of runway?
  5. Can Nigeria/Uganda water wells be independently verified as operational 90+ days post-installation?
  6. Why do multiple Palestine monthly tranches show identical $12,000 costs regardless of beneficiary variation?
  7. What is the strategic rationale for USA, Australia, and Turkiye as full branches?
  8. Are we legally registered to distribute aid in every listed country?
